# PROJECT.md — Awesome AI Idea Engine

## 1. Project name

```text
Awesome AI Idea Engine
```

Recommended GitHub repository name:

```text
awesome-ai-idea-engine
```

Recommended GitHub description:

```text
A curated engine of practical AI ideas for products, agents, automations, services, startups, and internal tools.
```

Recommended tagline:

```text
Discover, structure, score, validate, and contribute practical AI ideas — without hype or income promises.
```

## 2. Project vision

Awesome AI Idea Engine is an open-source, structured, and community-driven repository designed to collect, organize, score, and enrich practical AI ideas.

The project should not be a shallow list of random prompts or vague “AI startup ideas.” It should be a structured idea discovery engine that helps builders, consultants, freelancers, product teams, founders, students, and innovation teams explore useful AI-powered opportunities.

The repository should focus on this path:

```text
Idea → Target user → Pain point → AI solution → Use case → Stack → Business model → Difficulty → Validation step → Risk
```

The project should help users answer:

```text
What AI ideas are worth exploring?
Who would use them?
What problem do they solve?
What kind of AI workflow is involved?
How difficult would they be to build?
What business model could fit?
How should the idea be validated?
What are the risks?
```

The repository should be easy to browse, easy to contribute to, and easy to generate or update automatically.

## 3. What this project is

Awesome AI Idea Engine is:

- A structured database of AI ideas.
- A practical AI idea discovery system.
- A source of inspiration for builders and product teams.
- A contribution-friendly GitHub repository.
- A curated and scored catalog of AI product, service, agent, automation, and internal-tool ideas.
- A bridge between raw ideas and validated opportunities.
- A dataset that can later power a website, search engine, API, dashboard, newsletter, or AI assistant.
- A sober alternative to hype-driven “AI business idea” lists.

## 4. What this project is not

This project is not:

- Financial advice.
- A promise of income.
- A startup success predictor.
- A passive income guide.
- A list of guaranteed business ideas.
- A low-quality prompt dump.
- A viral “side hustle” list.
- An affiliate directory.
- A spam automation guide.
- A substitute for customer discovery.
- A substitute for legal, privacy, security, or compliance review.

Every idea should be treated as a hypothesis that requires validation.

## 5. Core positioning

The repository should be positioned around this statement:

```text
A practical, structured, and anti-hype idea engine for discovering useful AI-powered opportunities.
```

The words “Idea Engine” imply:

- Discovery.

The project should be more dynamic and browseable than a traditional handbook.

## 6. Target audiences

### 6.1 Indie builders

People looking for AI product, micro-SaaS, automation, plugin, browser extension, or API ideas.

### 6.2 Freelancers and consultants

People looking for AI service ideas, productized offers, automation packages, RAG services, or implementation opportunities.

### 6.3 Startup founders

People exploring early-stage AI-native ideas and market opportunities.

### 6.4 Product managers and innovation teams

People looking for AI use cases to evaluate inside companies.

### 6.5 Students and learners

People who want practical project ideas to build and learn AI product development.

People who want project ideas that connect technical AI capabilities to real workflows.

### 6.7 Community contributors

People who want to suggest, tag, enrich, or review AI ideas.

## 7. Editorial principles

The repository must prioritize clarity, usefulness, and specificity.

### 7.1 Required principles

Every idea should be:

- Specific.
- Practical.
- Short enough to browse.
- Structured enough to evaluate.
- Connected to a target user.
- Connected to a real pain point or workflow.
- Clear about the AI role.
- Clear about difficulty.
- Clear about validation.
- Clear about risks.
- Free from unrealistic claims.

### 7.2 Forbidden editorial patterns

Do not publish ideas that use or imply:

- Guaranteed income.
- Passive income without effort.
- Easy money.
- “Make $10k/month with AI.”
- No-skill businesses.
- Fake urgency.
- Fake scarcity.
- Spam automation.
- Deceptive outreach.
- Phishing.
- Deepfake scams.
- Unauthorized scraping.
- Content farms designed to manipulate search engines.
- Privacy-invasive monitoring.
- Automated high-stakes decisions without human oversight.
- Medical, legal, financial, employment, or public-service decisions without compliance warnings and human review.

### 7.3 Preferred language

Prefer:

```text
Target user
Pain point
Idea summary
AI workflow
Business model hypothesis
Difficulty
Validation step
Risks
Tags
Source
Status
```

Avoid:

```text

Easy money
Passive AI income
Cash machine
Secret method
No effort
Get rich with AI
```

## 12. Idea status lifecycle

```text
discovered
candidate
needs_review
needs_more_sources
needs_validation
accepted
rejected
duplicate
deprecated
```

Definitions:

- `discovered`: Automatically found from a source.
- `candidate`: Generated or submitted but not reviewed.
- `needs_review`: Requires maintainer review.
- `needs_more_sources`: Interesting but insufficiently supported.
- `needs_validation`: Useful but lacks a validation path.
- `accepted`: Curated and included.
- `rejected`: Not suitable for the project.
- `duplicate`: Similar to an existing idea.
- `deprecated`: Previously accepted but outdated or no longer recommended.

## 17. Scoring framework

The project should score ideas to help prioritization, not to predict success.

### 17.1 Scoring dimensions

```yaml
scoring_dimensions:
  pain_intensity:
    description: "How painful, frequent, and costly is the problem?"
    scale: "1 = weak pain, 5 = strong pain"

  ai_fit:
    description: "Does AI meaningfully improve the workflow?"
    scale: "1 = weak fit, 5 = strong fit"

  buildability:
    description: "Can a small team or individual build a useful MVP?"
    scale: "1 = hard to build, 5 = easy to build"

  market_signal:
    description: "Are there public signals that people care about this problem?"
    scale: "1 = weak signal, 5 = strong signal"

  differentiation_potential:
    description: "Can the idea become differentiated through workflow, data, niche, trust, integration, or domain expertise?"
    scale: "1 = weak, 5 = strong"

  monetization_potential:
    description: "Is there a plausible way to capture value?"
    scale: "1 = unclear, 5 = strong"

  compliance_risk:
    description: "Are there legal, privacy, security, ethical, or regulatory risks?"
    scale: "1 = low, 5 = high"

  saturation_risk:
    description: "How crowded is the market?"
    scale: "1 = uncrowded, 5 = very crowded"
```

### 17.2 Idea priority score

Use a transparent heuristic:

```text
idea_priority_score =
  + 2.0 * pain_intensity
  + 1.8 * ai_fit
  + 1.5 * buildability
  + 1.2 * market_signal
  + 1.2 * differentiation_potential
  + 1.0 * monetization_potential
  - 0.8 * compliance_risk
  - 0.7 * saturation_risk
```

This score is not a prediction of revenue, success, funding, or adoption. It is a prioritization aid.

  - id: structured

  - id: enriched
    name: Enriched Idea

  - id: validated
    name: Validated Idea
    description: Has evidence from interviews, prototypes, public signals, or market tests.

  - id: deprecated
    name: Deprecated Idea

The repository should prioritize structured and enriched ideas over raw volume.

## 22. Daily or weekly update system

The project must include an automated discovery and update pipeline.

The goal is to discover new idea candidates from public sources, normalize them, score them, and submit them for human review.

Automation should not directly publish ideas as accepted.

### 22.1 Recommended frequency

Default:

```text
Weekly update every Monday at 07:00 UTC
```

Optional:

```text
Daily update every day at 07:00 UTC
```

Weekly updates are better for quality. Daily updates are useful for fast-moving discovery.

### 22.2 Update pipeline

```text
Discover → Normalize → Deduplicate → Generate candidates → Enrich → Classify → Score → Human review → Merge
```

Detailed steps:

1. Read configured sources from `data/sources.yaml`.
2. Fetch recent resources, tools, products, posts, repositories, templates, and marketplace entries.
3. Normalize metadata:
   - name
   - URL
   - description
   - source
   - date discovered
   - tags
   - popularity signal, if available
4. Deduplicate by canonical URL, normalized title, GitHub repository, domain, or fuzzy similarity.
5. Generate idea candidates from useful resources.
6. Enrich candidates with:
   - target users
   - pain points
   - AI workflow
   - possible business models
   - technical stack
   - validation steps
   - risks
7. Classify into category, industry, role, AI pattern, and difficulty.
8. Score candidates.
9. Write outputs to:
   - `generated/latest-ideas.md`
   - `generated/new-idea-candidates.md`
   - `generated/pending-review.md`
   - `generated/trend-signals.md`
   - `data/updates_log.yaml`
10. Open a pull request for human review.
11. Maintainers decide whether to accept, reject, merge, or enrich candidates.

### 22.3 Candidate lifecycle

```text
discovered
candidate
needs_review
needs_more_sources
needs_validation
accepted
rejected
duplicate
deprecated
```

## 28. Contribution guidelines

`CONTRIBUTING.md` should include:

### 28.1 Accepted contributions

- New AI ideas.
- Idea enrichment.
- New sources.
- New generator prompts.
- New validation methods.
- Scoring improvements.
- Tag improvements.
- Risk and ethics reviews.
- Duplicate detection.
- Deprecation reports.
- Broken link fixes.

### 28.2 Quality requirements

Contributions must be:

- Practical.
- Specific.
- Clear.
- Honest.
- Target-user oriented.
- Pain-point oriented.
- Internationally understandable.
- Clear about what AI does.
- Clear about validation.
- Clear about risks and limitations.
- Free from income guarantees.
- Free from affiliate or promotional bias.

### 28.3 Rejection reasons

A contribution may be rejected if it is:

- Too vague.
- Too promotional.
- Misleading.
- Duplicated.
- Unsupported by sources when factual claims are made.
- Ethically problematic.
- Legally risky.
- Mostly affiliate content.
- Based on unrealistic income claims.
- Designed primarily for spam or platform abuse.
- Not meaningfully related to AI.

### 28.4 Sensitive domains

For healthcare, finance, insurance, legal, employment, education evaluation, public services, security, and law enforcement, include:

```text
This use case may involve regulated or high-stakes decisions. Human review, compliance assessment, security controls, privacy review, and domain expertise are required before production use.
```

Build the repository as a credible, international, anti-hype AI idea engine.
